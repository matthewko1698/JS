Prep 4-4-19: Force Diagrams

Dataset 1: Chemical Bonds

The first dataset that could easily be represented as a force diagram is chemical structures. Specifically, I would like to be able to visualize hydrates. These are structures that contain water molecules as part of their makeup. Silicon Dioxide Hydrate is not only a bunch of silicon, oxygen, and hydrogen connected, but the H2O in the structure actually stays together and can be removed even. The nodes for this diagram can be set as the molecules or atoms. The edges represent the bonds between them. The strength of these bonds will be interesting because the bonds within the water molecule will be stronger than the bonds between the water molecule and the silicon and oxygen. Bonds strength has been quantified by bond energy, the amount of energy it takes to break the bonds.

Dataset 2: Language

The second specific piece of data that can work well with force diagrams are linguistic pieces of data. Every human body is able to make the same sounds from our voice boxes but the environment we grow up in develops how we learn how to use it. The different specific sounds we can make can be mapped by language and use throughout the world. The nodes can represent the specific sounds and colors can represent what languages utilize them. Then the edges can represent how close these sounds are to one another, thereby making the relationship on how similar different languages are to each other with the sounds they produce. Stronger edges represent strong similarities

Dataset 3: Social Network

A social network profile dataset would fit very well with a force diagram. If we have a group of friends' social media profiles, we can figure out who is following who and how often they interact with other profiles. The more likes or comments another individual gives another can represent the strength of the edge that will connect the two nodes, profiles in this case. Data over time can show how friendships fade or grow as edges grow weaker or stronger.

Dataset 4: Scheduling Conflicts

If there are hundreds of congressmen on multiple committees each, what is the best way of scheduling the meetings such that the most congressmen can attend each meeting? Because there is a limited amount of time in the day there is only a fixed amount of schedules available and all congressmen will not be able to go to every one of their committee meetings. If you make a force diagram, with committee meetings as the nodes and edges representing a conflict (commitee memmber on both), then you can find the best way to schedule the meetings. You could find the shortest path that connects all the meetings to move them to a different day or hour.
